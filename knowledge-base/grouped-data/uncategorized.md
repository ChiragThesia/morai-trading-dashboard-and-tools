## Group Summary: Trading Terms Reference

### Overview
This is a comprehensive alphabetical glossary of 150+ trading terms spanning options, futures, equities, and derivatives trading. It covers foundational vocabulary (bid/ask spreads, long/short, strike prices, expiration), account types and structures (cash accounts, margin accounts, CFD accounts, proprietary firm accounts), technical analysis tools (candlesticks, moving averages, RSI, MACD, Bollinger Bands), options strategies (spreads, straddles, butterfly spreads, naked puts/calls), leverage and risk concepts (margin calls, margin requirements, shorting mechanics, dark pools), and market mechanics (market makers, ECNs, settlement timelines, trading hours, circuit breakers). The glossary is focused on US equities and derivatives trading with explicit callouts for regulatory contexts (PDT rules, pattern day trader minimums) and international differences (CFDs illegal in US, overseas trading rules).

### Key Insights
- **Foundational vocabulary separates competent traders from reckless ones**: Definitions for bid/ask spread, bid/ask price, ATM (at-the-money), ITM (in-the-money), and OTM (out-of-the-money) are essential because they directly affect position mechanics. A trader who doesn't understand that selling a put requires enough cash to buy 100 shares at strike price, or that a spread's maximum loss is the difference between strikes minus premium collected, will inevitably make expensive mistakes.

- **Account structure determines what trading is possible and what risks exist**: Cash accounts require T+3 settlement; margin accounts allow intraday leverage (4x in US, 6x at SureTrader, 10x+ at prop firms) and day trading; CFD accounts (illegal in US) offer extreme leverage up to 50x but create counterparty risk. The glossary explicitly notes these differences, showing that traders must understand their account constraints before choosing strategies. A wheel trader needs margin; an iron condor seller might use a cash account if they close positions before settlement.

- **Leverage amplifies both profits and losses catastrophically**: The glossary defines leverage, margin calls, and days-to-cover, emphasizing that 4x intraday leverage with a $100k balance creates $400k buying power, but a $250k position leaves only $150k remaining. The glossary's margin call definition clarifies the variation margin concept: losers pay cash daily, winners collect cash daily, forcing traders to manage intraday swings. A downside move of 25% on a leveraged position can trigger a margin call and forced liquidation.

- **Technical analysis vocabulary (candlesticks, moving averages, RSI, MACD, Bollinger Bands) represents tactical entry/exit tools rather than standalone trading systems**: Definitions emphasize that candlesticks contain 4 data points (open, close, high, low), moving averages weight recent price action (exponential moving averages more than simple moving averages), and indicators like RSI (0-100 scale with extremes at 0 and 100) identify overbought/oversold conditions. None of these are sufficient alone; the glossary notes that candlestick patterns combined with RSI provide more confidence than RSI alone.

- **Market structure concepts (dark pools, ECNs, smart routing, market makers) directly affect execution quality and cost**: Dark pools hold shares for institutions and trade internally out of sight, creating information asymmetries where retail traders can't access liquidity that institutions can. ECNs (electronic communication networks) and market makers create spreads and charge "tolls" (fees) for routing orders. Smart routing (broker-selected best route) may prioritize the broker's revenue, not the trader's best price. The glossary reveals that understanding market mechanics is essential to understanding why identical orders on identical securities can fill at different prices.

- **Risk concepts permeate the glossary and require careful integration**: Definitions cover Value at Risk (VAR), volatility measures (Average True Range, Bollinger Bands), short interest (leading to short squeezes), circuit breakers (trading halts), and stop-loss orders. A trader who understands only strikes and premiums but not VAR, volatility regimes, or short squeeze mechanics will be blindsided. The glossary shows that risk management requires integrating multiple concepts—not just position sizing, but also understanding the distribution of outcomes, regime changes, and market structure mechanics.

- **Regulatory rules and constraints are embedded in trading mechanics**: The glossary defines Pattern Day Trader (PDT) rules (3+ day trades in 5 days triggers $25,000 minimum account requirement), Short Sale Restriction (SSR) mechanics (10%+ daily drop prevents shorting except on upticks), settlement timelines (T+3 for stocks, T+1 for options, immediate for certain international ETFs on commission-free routes), and borrowing constraints (brokers may run out of shares to short, preventing short positions entirely). These are not optional considerations—they directly restrict which strategies are possible when.

### Key Questions
- How should traders integrate technical analysis (candlestick patterns, moving averages, RSI) with fundamental concepts like volatility (ATR, Bollinger Bands) to construct coherent entry/exit rules that work across different market regimes?
- What is the relationship between the market structure concepts (dark pools, ECNs, smart routing, market maker spreads) and the actual slippage and transaction costs that wheel traders and iron condor sellers incur when rolling positions?
- How do regulatory constraints (PDT rules, SSR, borrowing availability, settlement timelines, circuit breaker halts) interact with leverage and margin calls to create scenarios where traders are forced to exit at exactly the wrong time?

### Major Patterns & Themes
- **Vocabulary reveals hidden complexity**: Each definition, when fully understood, points to a decision point or risk factor. "Bid-ask spread" seems simple, but reveals that traders always buy at ask (higher price) and sell at bid (lower price), locking in a loss on entry. "Margin call" seems like a liquidation warning, but reveals daily cash flows that can force intraday management and position liquidation at the worst times.

- **Leverage is a double-edged sword that appears throughout the glossary**: Margin amplifies returns (4x your capital becomes $400k in buying power on $100k cash), but also amplifies losses (25% decline wipes out your entire margin). CFD accounts offer 50x leverage but create counterparty risk. Proprietary firm accounts offer 10x+ leverage to licensed traders but require capital deposits and pass stringent regulatory exams. The glossary emphasizes that leverage is accessible to different traders at different cost/complexity levels.

- **The glossary prioritizes US equities and options but includes derivatives, futures, and international trading**: Definitions cover calls, puts, spreads, straddles, collars, and covered calls (all options), but also futures contracts, forwards, CFDs, and swaps. This breadth shows that traders can apply similar mechanics (strike, expiration, premium) across asset classes, but each has structural differences (e.g., futures require daily variation margin settlement; options decay by theta; CFDs create counterparty exposure).

- **Risk management is built into the glossary's structure**: Definitions for stop-loss orders, trailing stops, diversification, dollar-cost averaging, hedges, and Value at Risk (VAR) appear alongside offensive concepts (leverage, arbitrage, short squeezes, gaps), emphasizing that surviving in trading requires both offensive and defensive skills. A trader cannot win with good ideas alone; they must manage position sizing, stop losses, and portfolio construction to avoid catastrophic losses.
