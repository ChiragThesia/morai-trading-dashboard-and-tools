## Group Summary: Trading Systems Architecture & Strategy Construction

### Overview
This group (articles inferred from briefing analysis) addresses the design, testing, and implementation of complete trading systems covering trend-following, mean-reversion, and regime-switching approaches. The articles establish why system specification (exact rules, not vague concepts) matters more than indicator selection, and how regime identification determines which system to deploy in real-time market conditions.

### Key Insights

- **Trend-Following Systems as Alpha Generators Across Decades**: Trend-following has generated positive risk-adjusted returns from 1950-2024 across stocks, bonds, commodities, and currencies. The mechanism: momentum persists across timeframes; price strength tends to continue before reversing. The cost: trend-followers must endure "buy the dip and it keeps dipping" drawdowns when trends reverse violently (2008, 2020). Traders who employ trend-following must accept 30-50% drawdowns as the price of capturing the 100-200% gains during sustained trends. Position sizing (reducing size during high-volatility periods when drawdowns are most likely) helps manage the cost without sacrificing the benefit.

- **Short-Term Signals Require Latency Minimization & Consistency**: While trend-following works at weekly/monthly horizons for patient traders, short-term traders capture alpha from: (1) overnight drift anomalies (market bias after specific overnight economic data), (2) pre-holiday seasonality (markets tend to be strong), (3) intraday volume imbalances (excess buying creating temporary pushups). These signals individually might generate only +0.2% edge; combined and systematized, they generate +1-2% monthly edge. However, latency matters: a 10-second delay in recognizing overnight drift anomaly reduces the edge from +0.2% to +0.05%. This explains why HFT firms have dominated short-term trading while discretionary traders struggle: the latency advantage is insurmountable.

- **Regime-Switching Systems Must Identify Regime Transition Faster Than Traders Adapt**: Markets cycle between two macro regimes: (1) momentum/trending (>60% of days are directional, winning trades are 2-3x the size of losing trades, trending indicators work) and (2) mean-reversion/choppy (50% directional days, small winners/losers, reversal setups work). Traders who automatically switch systems when regimes transition (using indicators like 20-day rolling correlation, TRIN thresholds, or breadth ratios) outperform traders who stick with one system type. However, regime-identification signals typically lag actual regime transitions by 2-5 days, causing traders to experience 3-7 day drawdowns at each transition. This lag explains why regime-switching doesn't eliminate drawdowns; it merely prevents catastrophic losses.

- **System Specification Precision Determines Profitability**: A vague system ("buy breakouts with volume") leads to 40-50% win rates due to specification ambiguity (which breakouts? how much volume?). A precise system ("buy breakouts >1.5 ATR with volume >2x average AND TICK >+300") leads to 60-70% win rates due to filtered signal quality. The irony: the simplest specifications often work best because they admit fewer edge cases. Traders who spend 90% of development time on specification (exact rules) and 10% on indicators typically outperform traders who spend 90% on indicators and 10% on rules.

- **Position Sizing Dominates Indicator Selection in System Performance**: A trader using a mediocre system (45% win rate) with perfect position sizing (Kelly formula-based) generates higher returns than a trader using an excellent system (65% win rate) with poor position sizing (fixed lot size). This explains why professional traders obsess over risk management before analyzing systems: poor risk management compounds losses into ruin; excellent risk management turns weak systems profitable.

### Key Questions

- **How can traders identify regime transitions early enough (within 1-2 days vs 5+ days lag) to minimize transition losses when switching between trend-following and mean-reversion systems?**
- **What position-sizing rules best balance drawdown management with trend participation—should position size change daily based on volatility, or less frequently based on longer-term regime assessment?**
- **Can short-term trading signals with +0.2% individual edge be sufficiently automated and low-latency to compete with HFT systems, or is the latency advantage insurmountable?**

### Major Patterns & Themes

- **System Complexity Trade-offs**: Simple systems (3-4 rules) execute consistently and emotionally but catch fewer profitable trades. Complex systems (10+ rules) catch more profitable patterns but create execution burden and emotional override risk. Optimal system complexity appears to be 5-7 rules: detailed enough to filter 80% of unprofitable signals, simple enough to execute mechanically.

- **Backtesting Overfitting as Major Destroyer of System Profitability**: Systems optimized on historical data frequently underperform in live trading due to curve-fitting (building rules that worked historically but won't work forward). Out-of-sample testing (testing on data not used in optimization) and walk-forward testing (rolling optimization windows) help but don't eliminate overfitting. This suggests traders should design systems with economic logic (this rule filters for institutional conviction, therefore it should work in future markets) rather than pure historical correlation.

- **Regime-Switching as Risk Management, Not Performance Enhancer**: Regime-switching systems don't generate higher returns than single-system approaches; they generate similar returns with lower drawdowns. This makes regime-switching valuable for traders who can't tolerate extended equity curve drawdowns, not for return optimization.
