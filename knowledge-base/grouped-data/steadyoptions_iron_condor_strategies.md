## Group Summary: Iron Condor Strategies

### Key Insights
- Iron condors are short volatility strategies combining a bear call spread and bull put spread to profit from stagnating or slightly directional market movement, generating returns of 8-10% monthly in normal market conditions when properly managed. The strategy has high win rates (8-9 months per year typical) but requires strict discipline in allocation and exit rules, as the occasional losing month can produce 40-50% account losses without proper position sizing.
- The "10% per month" promise from many trading websites is mathematically possible but practically dangerous if it requires deploying 70-80% of trading capital into highly correlated index contracts (RUT, SPX, NDX). These indexes are 90% correlated, creating systemic risk rather than diversification—a single market collapse can wipe out the entire account, regardless of how many separate trades exist.
- Optimal iron condor management requires opening trades 6-8 weeks before expiration, closing 2-3 weeks before expiration to avoid gamma risk, and selecting strikes based on delta (typically 15-20 delta for short strikes yields 80-85% probability of profit). Never hold into expiration week due to accelerating gamma risk, and avoid trading iron condors when implied volatility (VIX) is depressed (below 15), as insufficient premium is available for the risk taken.
- Proper risk management means maintaining position allocation at 10-20% of total capital per trade, allowing the account to survive occasional large losses without catastrophic impact. Average losses should not exceed 25-30% of average wins; for example, if average winner is 15%, average loser should be limited to 25-30% through disciplined stop-loss management.

### Key Questions
- What is the minimum implied volatility level where iron condor risk/reward becomes attractive, and how should you adjust strikes and allocation when VIX is elevated versus depressed?
- How do you mechanically implement the rule of "close 2-3 weeks before expiration" while maintaining consistent profitability and dealing with varying volatility environments?
- Should you attempt to leg into iron condors by selling the put spread first and call spread second, or is simultaneous entry necessary to avoid directional timing risk?
