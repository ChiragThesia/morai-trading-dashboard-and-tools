## Group Summary: Straddle & Strangle Strategies

### Key Insights
- Straddles and strangles are long volatility strategies that profit from large price moves in either direction, making them ideal for earnings plays and earnings-driven market dislocations. A long straddle buys an ATM (at-the-money) call and put with identical strikes, while a long strangle buys OTM (out-of-the-money) call and put with different strikes, with strangles having lower cost but requiring larger price moves to profit.
- These strategies suffer from theta decay and implied volatility crush—the ideal entry point is days before an earnings announcement when IV is elevated, with immediate exit after the earnings announcement when IV collapses, even if the price move was in the desired direction. Holding through earnings announcement or beyond typically results in profits turning to losses due to IV crush despite favorable directional moves.
- Reverse iron condors (long strangles) are directional alternatives to straddles with defined maximum loss but theoretically unlimited maximum profit, suitable when expecting a specific large directional move (e.g., bullish earnings surprise) combined with elevated volatility. The tradeoff is lower probability of profit and higher upfront cost compared to short strangles.
- Backtesting pre-earnings straddles reveals that timing entry (when to buy), timing exit (when to sell), and selection of strike prices relative to expected move all significantly impact profitability. Many traders over-estimate the price move and buy straddles too far ITM (costing more premium) or exit too early before the full move is realized.

### Key Questions
- How do you estimate the expected move size from earnings to determine optimal strike selection (ATM for straddles versus different deltas for strangles)?
- What is the optimal timing for entry (days before earnings) and exit (immediately after announcement, next day, or multiple days later), and how does this vary by stock and earnings surprise magnitude?
- How should you position size long straddles versus short strangles given their inverse risk profiles, and when is each more appropriate given your volatility outlook?
