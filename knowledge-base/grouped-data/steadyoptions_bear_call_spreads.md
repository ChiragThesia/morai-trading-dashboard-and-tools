## Group Summary: Bear Call Spreads

### Key Insights
- Bear call spreads are credit spreads designed for moderately bearish to neutral market outlooks, allowing traders to profit from declining or stagnating stock prices with defined maximum risk and profit potential. The short call receives premium while the long call provides downside protection, creating a risk/reward profile suitable for directional trades during expected price declines.
- Risk is limited to the net premium paid for the spread, making position sizing predictable and manageable compared to naked short calls. Maximum profit is achieved when the stock closes at or below the lower strike price, equal to the spread width minus the premium paid.
- The strategy works effectively during high implied volatility periods when option premiums are elevated, allowing traders to sell calls at favorable prices. Delta selection is critical: selling puts at 15-30 delta typically provides 70-85% probability of profit, while maintaining adequate protective long calls to limit downside risk.
- Timing and selection of strike prices determine profitability; entering trades 4-6 weeks before expiration with enough spread width (at least $2-3) provides better risk/reward ratios than weekly trades with tight strikes.

### Key Questions
- How do you select optimal strike prices for bear call spreads to balance probability of profit (probability the trade expires worthless) against the premium received?
- What market conditions or volatility levels make bear call spreads most attractive versus other bearish strategies like simple short puts or long put spreads?
- How should position sizing change when building multiple bear call spreads across different underlying assets to manage portfolio-level risk?
