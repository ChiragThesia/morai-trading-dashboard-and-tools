## Group Summary: Risk Management

### Key Insights
- The fundamental rule of risk management is limiting any single position to 1% of total trading capital as maximum loss, creating psychological and financial discipline. This means if your account is $100,000, the maximum loss on one position cannot exceed $1,000; options strategies allow precise definition of this maximum loss through spread design and strike selection.
- Options provide a critical advantage over stock trading: maximum loss can be predetermined before entering any trade through strategy design. Long calls and puts have limited maximum loss (premium paid), spreads have defined maximum losses (spread width minus credits received), and properly managed short strategies use protective collars—unlike stock positions where gaps at market open can obliterate planned risk limits.
- Trading psychology and emotional discipline matter far more than technical analysis (90% psychology vs 10% technique by many estimates). Risk management protects traders from making fear-based or greed-driven decisions by creating predefined rules that remove emotion—the trader follows the plan rather than reacting to price movement and news.
- Position allocation rules should prevent correlated losses from destroying an account. Never allocate more than 20-30% to a single strategy, and avoid concentrated positions in highly correlated underlyings (broad market indexes like SPX, RUT, NDX moving in tandem). Proper diversification across uncorrelated assets, strategies, and time frames prevents account-ending losses.

### Key Questions
- How do you mechanically implement the "1% rule" across multiple positions simultaneously, especially when positions move and the maximum loss changes over time?
- What specific events or market conditions (earnings surprises, gap events, Fed decisions) should trigger position exits regardless of unrealized loss or profit?
- How should risk management rules adapt when trading weekly versus monthly versus quarterly options with different volatility and time decay profiles?
